4-step boundary script

  1. Connect first. Name what your child wants or feels.
  2. State the limit. Use one calm sentence.
  3. Give the reason. Keep it short, usually safety, health, respect, or family rhythm.
  4. Offer the next step. Give one choice, one action, or one repair path.

Simple formula: “You really want ____. The limit is ____. This matters because ____. You can ____ or ____.”

Boundary planner

  • Situation: What keeps repeating?
  • Boundary: What is the clear rule?
  • Kind words: How will you validate the feeling?
  • Follow-through: What happens every time?
  • Repair: How will you reconnect after the hard part?

Everyday examples

Screen transition
“You’re having fun. The screen is done now. Your brain needs a break. You can put it on the charger or I can help.”
Hitting
“You’re angry. I won’t let you hit. Hands stay safe. You can stomp your feet or squeeze this pillow.”
Leaving the park
“You wish we could stay. It’s time to go home for dinner. Do you want to walk to the car or hold my hand and hop?”
Teen curfew
“I get that you want more time. The curfew is still 10:30 tonight. Show me a plan for check-ins and we can revisit it next weekend.”

What to avoid

  • Long lectures while your child is dysregulated.
  • Changing the rule because the protest got louder.
  • Using shame, labels, or sarcasm.
  • Offering too many choices when the limit is not optional.
  • Skipping repair because you feel guilty or tired.

Printable checklists

Print the whole page or copy these into your notes app.

Weekly parent reset

  • Did my child feel seen this week?
  • Did we protect sleep, meals, movement, and connection?
  • Did I set one clear boundary and hold it calmly?
  • Did I repair after a hard moment?
  • Did I notice one strength my child is building?

Before reacting

  • Pause and lower your voice.
  • Name what you see.
  • Validate the feeling without giving up the boundary.
  • Offer one next step.
  • Reconnect after the moment passes.

Kind words, firm boundaries

  • Connect: “I see you really want this.”
  • Limit: “The rule is still the rule.”
  • Reason: “This keeps us safe, healthy, or respectful.”
  • Next step: “You can choose A or B.”
  • Repair: “That was hard. We’re okay. Let’s try again.”

Family values check

  • What do we want our child to feel at home?
  • What routines protect that feeling?
  • Which battles are not worth having?
  • Where do we need more consistency?

Digital safety check

  • No screens replace sleep, meals, play, school, or friendships.
  • Use shared spaces for younger kids.
  • Discuss content, privacy, ads, bullying, and consent.
  • Model the phone habits you want them to learn.

When to seek professional support

Most development has a wide normal range. Get support when your gut says something is off, when stress is persistent, or when daily life is becoming harder for your child or family.

Reach out to a pediatrician, family doctor, child psychologist, therapist, speech-language pathologist, occupational therapist, dietitian, or school support team when needed.

  • Loss of skills your child previously had.
  • Feeding, sleep, breathing, growth, or safety concerns.
  • Delayed speech, limited social connection, or major sensory distress.
  • Frequent aggression, self-harm talk, extreme anxiety, depression, isolation, or school refusal.
  • Family stress, trauma, grief, conflict, burnout, or caregiver depression.
